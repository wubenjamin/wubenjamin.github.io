About Me

Welcome! I am an active researcher who uses scientific computing and machine learning to solve problems in the fields of astrophysics and space exploration.

Currently, I work at the National Astronomical Observatory of Japan (NAOJ) as a postdoctoral fellow aiming to advance humankind’s understanding of how star clusters and stellar systems form. I develop and analyze supercomputer simulations of interstellar gas clouds in order to bridge astrophysical theory with observational data from the groundbreaking Atacama Large Millimeter Array (ALMA).

I also recently participated in the NASA Frontier Development Lab<\a>, an artificial intelligence research accelerator established to apply advanced machine learning techniques to current challenges in space exploration. There, I coupled machine vision with convolutional neural networks to help planetary rovers localize themselves in environments where GPS is absent, such as the moon and Mars.

Previously, I completed my Ph.D. in Physics at the University of Florida and my B.S. in Physics and Mathematics at Duke University. When I am neither computing clouds nor cloud computing, you can find me on the rocks at a local climbing gym, in a hole-in-the-wall izakaya, or helping out the Kerbal space program.

Research

I study the collapse of interstellar gas into stars by developing and running magnetohydrodynamics simulations on supercomputers. Using these multi-scale, multi-physics simulations, we can make predictions to compare with and better understand star-forming regions observed within galaxies. I'm especially interested the role of interstellar magnetic fields, turbulence, and shockwaves in triggering the formation of star clusters.

I use numerical simulations of astrophysical fluids to investigate the problem of how stars form. This fundamental process directs the evolution of galaxies, the structure of interstellar gas, and the creation of planetary systems. Despite its universal importance, our understanding of star formation is surprisingly limited due to the complex, nonlinear mechanisms at play within the interstellar medium (ISM). Gravity, turbulence, magnetic fields, stellar feedback, radiation, chemistry, and microphysical heating/cooling processes all influence how the available gas from molecular clouds collapses into stars. Below are some of my recent research highlights and scientific contributions.

  • Cluster-Forming Clumps

    Most stars form in clusters from clumps within molecular clouds. Obtaining properties of clumps (e.g., densities, temperatures, velocities, magnetic fields) is observationally challenging and often reveals complex, multi-component structures. I have been developing sophisticated numerical models of gravitationally infalling and rotating clumps with turbulence, magnetic fields, and star formation (Wu et al., in prep).

  • Numerical Star Formation

    Numerical treatments of star formation using sub-grid models generally do not take magnetic pressure support into account when creating stars. We have developed new sub-grid star formation routines regulated via magnetic field criticality and have modeled the creation and early evolution of such star clusters (Wu et al. 2017b). This offers an additional avenue of comparison to young star clusters and their natal gas.

  • Collision-Driven Turbulence

    Cloud collisions are an attractive star cluster formation triggering mechanism due to the way they naturally accumulate large amounts of molecular gas into dense filamentary regions within short timescales. In addition to connecting galactic orbital dynamics at kiloparsec scales to star cluster formation at parsec scales and explaining the high dispersion in star formation efficiencies, they also may be a source of turbulence driving in molecular clouds (Wu, et al. 2018).

  • Magnetic Field Signatures

    The role of interstellar magnetic fields is not well-understood. However, increasing evidence suggests they may significantly influence molecular cloud evolution and thus star formation. Magnetic fields are central in our numerical framework, and we utilize novel magnetic polarization analysis methods as potential observational diagnostics (Wu et al. 2017a) which we can compare against dust polarization observations (e.g., Planck).

  • Heating & Cooling Functions

    Accurately capturing the multi-phase nature of the ISM motivated the development of rigorous gas heating/cooling functions based on the chemistry and microphysics that occur in photo-dissociation regions (PDRs) (Wu et al. 2015). These improve upon more commonly used isothermal or polytropic equations of state by realistically modeling gas thermal properties, predicting atomic and molecular abundances, and tracers used to observationally characterize regions within the ISM.

  • Line Emission Signatures

    By coupling our heating/cooling functions with the numerical code, we can not only model various astrophysical scenarios, but also predict species abundances as well as key observational tracers (e.g., excitation levels of 13CO, 12CO, [CII], and [OI]). The ability to create self-consistent synthetic observations to directly compare to data is a key advantage of the heating/cooling functions and has resulted in determining new line diagnostics for cloud collision triggered star formation (e.g., Wu et al. 2015, 2017a; Bisbas et al. 2017, 2018).

Outreach

It is a privilege to share the excitement of astronomy and astrophysics with people of all ages and backgrounds wherever I go. Here are various public outreach events in which I have helped organize.

(2016-Present) Mitaka Open House Day, National Astronomical Observatory of Japan
This annual event highlights space science research and education from four leading institutes in Japan: NAOJ; the Astrobiology Center; the University of Tokyo; and the Graduate University for Advanced Studies (SOKENDAI). Thousands of guests from the greater Tokyo area visit NAOJ during this festive weekend event. I have assisted and maintained the 3D planetarium "4D2U" experience as an international staff member. (#naojday!)
(2016) After Hours Adventure: Star Party, Kennedy Space Center Visitor Complex
This high profile event held at NASA's Kennedy Space Center combined educational astronomy and stargazing activities culminating with the peaking Perseid meteor shower. The UF Astronomy outreach team led interactive stations and set up telescopes for this family-friendly event exploring astronomy and space technology themes.
(2016) Springs and Stars, various Florida state parks
In this series of outreach events established by the UF Astronomy outreach team, we utilize Florida state parks to provide inspirational, interactive, and educational astronomy and stargazing activities in order to connect with underserved rural areas. Access and engagement with science is for everyone!
(2011-2015) Starry Night, Florida Museum of Natural History
The flagship outreach event run by the UF Astronomy Department, Starry Night provides an evening filled with astronomical activities including inflatable planetariums, 3D journeys through the galaxy, crawling through cosmic time, and much more. I've participated in building the exhibits, running the stations, and filming promotional content.
